Demystifying Codecs: What's the Best Format for the Web?

Published on June 29, 2025 • 5 min read

Have you ever finished an incredible edit, exported the video, and the final file was 5GB? Or maybe you uploaded a video to a website and it took forever to load? The answer to these problems almost always lies in a technical and often misunderstood word: the codec.

In this guide, we will demystify the world of video codecs and formats. The goal is not to delve into complex technical details, but to give you the practical knowledge to make the best choice when exporting your video for the web, finding the perfect balance between quality and performance.

Section 1: The Crucial Difference - Container vs. Codec

Think of a video file as a gift box.

The container is the box itself. It can have different shapes and colors, but its function is to hold everything inside: the video, the audio, subtitles, etc. When you see a file named video.mp4, .MP4 is the type of box.

The codec is the method by which the gift was folded and wrapped inside to fit in the box. Coder/Decoder. It is the 'language' or compression technique used to make the video file small enough to be stored and transmitted over the internet.

Thus, you can have an .MP4 box that contains a video 'wrapped' with the H.264 codec, or the same .MP4 box with a video 'wrapped' with the H.265 codec. The box is the container; the way it's packed is the codec.

Section 2: Delivery Codecs (For the Web and Streaming)

H.264 (or AVC) - The King of Compatibility

If you need your video to play on virtually any device on the planet—from old mobile phones to Smart TVs and all browsers—H.264 is the safe choice. It's not the most efficient, but it is the universal standard.

When to use: For maximum compatibility, videos for social media, and emails.

H.265 (or HEVC) - The Efficient Successor

This is the evolution of H.264. It can deliver the same image quality with a file that is about 40-50% smaller. It is the preferred codec for 4K streaming, like on Netflix.

When to use: For high-quality videos on your portfolio or website, where you know your audience will be using more modern devices.

AV1 - The Future is Open and Free

This is the most modern codec on the list, created by giants like Google, Apple, and Amazon. It is even more efficient than H.265 and, most importantly, it is open-source and royalty-free. YouTube already uses it extensively.

When to use: It is the best choice for the future. Today, it is excellent for YouTube videos and for those who want maximum compression with the best quality.

Section 3: Editing Codecs (Why Not Use Them on the Web?)

What about Apple ProRes, which my editor asked for? And Avid DNxHD? It's crucial to understand that these are editing (or intermediate) codecs. Their purpose is to preserve the maximum image quality during post-production, with very little compression. The result is gigantic files, perfect for working on the editing timeline without losing data, but completely impractical for loading on a website.

Conclusion: So, Which One to Use? (The Practical Summary)

  • For Maximum Compatibility (The Safe Choice): Use H.264 inside an .MP4 container. It's the combination that never fails.
  • For the Best Quality on Your Portfolio: If you want to display a 4K video with the best possible appearance, H.265 (HEVC) is a fantastic choice, resulting in smaller files than H.264.
  • The Golden Rule of Workflow: Edit in a high-quality format (like ProRes, if the camera allows) and always export the final version for the web in H.264 or H.265.

Understanding codecs means moving beyond being just an editor to also becoming a media technician. It's the skill that ensures your creative work, in which you have invested so much time, is seen by the largest number of people, with the best quality and performance it deserves.
